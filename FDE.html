1 Historical Background
The Faraday Eﬀect is a phenomenon discovered by the English scientist Michael Faraday
in the early 1840s. It is a crucial concept in the ﬁeld of optics and electromagnetism.
Michael Faraday conducted a series of experiments at the Royal Institution in London
in the early 1840s to examine the interplay of light and magnetic ﬁelds. His experiments
were largely concerned with the inﬂuence of magnets on light polarization. He observed
that when a beam of plane-polarized light went through a transparent material put in
a magnetic ﬁeld, the plane of polarization of the light rotated. The Faraday Eﬀect is
named after this rotation of the plane of polarization. He discovered that the degree of
rotation of the plane of polarization was exactly related to the strength of the applied
magnetic ﬁeld and the route length of the light through the material.
The main equation that governs the Faraday eﬀect is-
θ = V Bl (1)
where θ is the angle of rotation (in radians), B is the magnetic ﬂux density in the direction
of propagation (in teslas), l is the length of the path (in meters) where the light and
magnetic ﬁeld interact and V is the Verdet constant for the material
Also the direction of rotation was aﬀected by the direction of the magnetic ﬁeld.
When a magnetic ﬁeld was applied in one direction, the plane of polarization rotated
in that direction, and when the ﬁeld was reversed, the rotation also reversed. He did
not fully comprehend the atomic and molecular principles that underpin the Faraday
Eﬀect. His observations, however, were critical in the creation of later theoretical mod-
els. The interaction between the magnetic moments of electrons in the material and
the applied magnetic ﬁeld was later discovered to be the primary cause of the Faraday
Eﬀect. This interaction changes the refractive index of the material for circularly polar-
ized light, causing the plane of polarization to rotate. The Faraday eﬀect plays a vital
role in the development of magneto-optical devices, such as the Faraday rotator, which
allows light of a speciﬁc polarization to pass through a material in the presence of a mag-
netic ﬁeld. Such devices are used in applications like optical isolators and modulators in
telecommunications.

3 Discussion
3.1 Results
• We verify the phenomenon of birefringence. And get a hands-on experience on how
light is aﬀected by magnetic ﬁelds. From this experiment, we can also conclude
that light has a magnetic ﬁeld component associated with it.
• For a glass rod and for a 532 nm wavelength light we get the verdet constant value
from the table-1 as (27.8401 ± 2.7631) rad/Tm and from the plot we get the
verdet constant as (29.2225 ± 2.3257) rad/Tm.
• For a glass rod and for a 650 nm wavelength light we get the verdet constant value
from the table-1 as (18.9863 ± 3.2062) rad/Tm and from the plot we get the
verdet constant as (17.9979 ± 2.4402) rad/Tm.
• From the values calculated both fro the table and the plots we see that within
the range of their uncertainty or error bars they overlap. Thus the experimental
calculated value and the model ﬁtted value from the plots agree with each other.
3.2 Sources of Error
• We mainly suspect the precision of the instruments as the major cause of the error.
• A better photo sector can also reduce the error.
• Minor displacement in the optical setup can also come into an error while taking
the measurement.
