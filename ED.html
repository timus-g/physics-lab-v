1 Historical Background
The exploration of electron beams and the fundamental nature of electrons has a rich his-
tory that stretches beyond the formal discovery of electrons themselves. In 1938, Michael
Faraday’s pioneering work marked one of the earliest instances of an electron beam. His
experiments, which involved evacuated tubes and the application of high voltage, created
a path for charged particles, though the electron as we now know it had not yet been
formally discovered.[1]
As we delve into the late 19th century, a cadre of scientists including Heinrich Geissler,
Julius Pl¨ucker[3], Eugen Goldstein, and William Crookes embarked on a journey into
the study of cathode rays. They explored various properties of these electron beams and
their behavior within vacuum tubes. Their observations set the stage for comprehending
the fundamental characteristics of these charged particles.
It was in 1897 that Sir J. J. Thomson conducted meticulous experiments, shedding light
on the particle nature of electrons. Through precise measurements of the mass-to-charge
ratio of the constituent corpuscles of cathode rays, he laid the foundation for understand-
ing electrons as distinct particles, thus marking a signiﬁcant milestone in the history of
particle physics.[4]
Amidst the quantum revolution that marked the early 20th century, Louis de Broglie’s
groundbreaking Ph.D. thesis introduced the revolutionary concept of wave-particle du-
ality. His theory proposed that all matter particles, including electrons, possess dual
properties, acting as both particles and waves. This idea fundamentally challenged clas-
sical physics and revolutionized our understanding of the subatomic world.

The Davisson-Germer electron diﬀraction experiments[2] in 1927 provided critical exper-
imental conﬁrmation of de Broglie’s wave-particle duality hypothesis. These experiments
directed electrons at a crystalline target, leading to the observation of diﬀraction patterns
akin to those found with X-rays. This pivotal conﬁrmation solidiﬁed the dual nature of
electrons, signifying a profound turning point in quantum physics.
In the subsequent years, the technique of electron diﬀraction underwent further reﬁne-
ment, becoming an invaluable tool in the qualitative structural determination of various
substances. The ability to utilize electrons for diﬀraction signiﬁcantly advanced the ﬁeld
of material science and contributed to the structural elucidation of numerous compounds,
marking yet another chapter in the multifaceted journey of electron exploration in science.

• In the experiment, the wave nature of electrons in an evacuated glass tube is demon-
strated by observing their diﬀraction by polycrystalline graphite. On the ﬂuorescent
screen of the tube one observes diﬀraction rings around a central spot on the axis
of the beam. The diameter of the rings D depends on the accelerator voltage (see
Fig. 1a). They are caused by diﬀraction of electrons at those lattice planes of the
microcrystals that satisfy the Bragg condition (see Fig. 1b):
2 · d · sinθ = n · λ (3)
θ: the Bragg angle, n: diﬀraction order,
d: distance between the lattice planes
• The diameter of the diﬀraction ring corresponding to the Bragg angle θ is given by:
D = 2 · L · tan2θ (4)

3 Discussion
• This experiment is a direct evidence for the demonstration for the wave nature of
the electron. Thus we see that matter has also wave nature.
• We also verify that wavelength of the electron beam changes with changing the
accelerating potential. Thus we get a source with variable wavelength.
