1 Historical Background
The GM (Geiger-Muller) counter experiment is closely connected to the history of ra-
dioactivity and the development of radiation detection devices. To understand the history
of GM counter, we should rst go back and understand how radioactivity was discovered.
In 1896, the study of radioactivity began with the discovery of radioisotopes by Henri
Becquerel. He observed that certain materials, like uranium salts, emitted rays that could
penetrate opaque objects.
The Geiger-Muller counter was invented by German physicists Hans Geiger and Walther
Muller in 1928. It was made with the objective of detecting ionized radiation. The main
idea was to build a gas-lled tube with an electrode, and when radiation entered the tube,
it would ionize the gas, allowing the radiation to be detected. The Geiger-Muller counter
works on the assumption that ionizing radiation, such as alpha, beta, and gamma rays,
can ionize atoms in a gas-lled chamber. The resultant ion pairs generate a detectable
electric current. With the emergence of nuclear technologies and the development of
nuclear weapons in the mid-twentieth century, the necessity for dependable radiation
detection systems became important. Geiger-Muller counters were widely used in a vari-
ety of applications, including scientic research, medical diagnostics, and nuclear power
plants, to monitor and measure radiation.
While the Geiger-Muller counter is still frequently used in radiation detection, techno-
logical advances have resulted in the creation of alternative types of detectors, such as
scintillation counters and semiconductor detectors, which oer increased sensitivity and
capabilities in specialized applications.The GM counter experiment is frequently used in
educational contexts to explain radiation detection concepts and to measure radiation
levels from diverse sources. It is critical in both understanding the fundamental nature
of radiation and maintaining safety in radiation-aected situations.

3 Conclusions and Discussion
Understanding Quenching and Dead Time: The investigation of quenching eects
and dead time in GM counter experiments helps us to recognize the limitations in
counting eciency and accuracy, shedding light on challenges in high radiation rate
measurements.
Critical Role of Statistical Parameters: Through the analysis of counting rate and
standard deviation in GM counter experiments, we gain insights into the aver-
age frequency of radiation events and the precision of our measurements, oering
a comprehensive understanding of the statistical aspects crucial for reliable data
interpretation.

intricacies of quenching and dead time but also equip researchers with the knowl-
edge to optimize counting conditions. This, coupled with a focus on statistical
parameters, allows for improved accuracy in measuring radiation levels, ensuring
the reliability of experimental outcomes in radiation detection studies.
